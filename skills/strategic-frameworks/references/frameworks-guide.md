# Strategic Frameworks: Detailed Reference Guide

This document provides comprehensive guidance on applying each strategic framework to feature and product evaluation decisions. Use it as a deep-dive companion to the main skill file.

---

## 1. Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers

### Full Explanation

Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers framework, introduced in his book *7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy*, identifies the only seven sources of durable competitive advantage that allow a business to sustain differential returns. Each "power" creates a barrier that competitors cannot easily overcome, either because the incumbent has a benefit the challenger cannot match or because the challenger faces a cost the incumbent does not. The seven powers are: Scale Economies (unit cost declines with volume), Network Effects (value increases with each additional user), Counter-Positioning (a new business model that incumbents cannot adopt without damaging their existing business), Switching Costs (the cost a customer incurs by moving to a competitor), Branding (durable attribution of higher value to a product based on reputation), Cornered Resource (exclusive access to a valuable resource such as talent, patents, or data), and Process Power (embedded organizational processes that are extremely difficult to replicate).

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

When evaluating a feature, ask which of the seven powers it creates or reinforces. A feature that increases switching costs (e.g., data lock-in, workflow integration) is strategically more valuable than one that provides temporary convenience. Features that amplify network effects (e.g., collaboration, sharing, marketplace dynamics) compound in value over time. Counter-positioning features are particularly potent in disruption scenarios where incumbents are structurally unable to respond. Prioritize features that establish or deepen at least one power.

### Key Questions

- Which of the 7 Powers does this feature create or strengthen?
- Does this feature raise switching costs for existing customers?
- Does the feature become more valuable as more users adopt it (network effects)?
- Would a competitor need to cannibalize their own business to replicate this (counter-positioning)?
- Does this feature leverage a resource only we have access to (cornered resource)?

### Red Flags

- The feature provides a temporary advantage that any competitor can replicate within a quarter.
- No identifiable power is created or reinforced.
- The feature commoditizes a previously differentiated area without creating a new power elsewhere.
- Investment is large but the resulting advantage is easily bypassed.

### Green Flags

- The feature directly creates or strengthens an identifiable power.
- Competitive response would require structural changes competitors are unlikely to make.
- Value compounds over time (network effects, data moats, process improvements).
- The feature leverages an exclusive asset or capability.

## 2. Porter's Five Forces

### Full Explanation

Michael Porter's Five Forces framework analyzes the structural attractiveness of an industry by examining five competitive forces: the threat of new entrants (how easy it is for new competitors to enter the market), the bargaining power of suppliers (how much leverage suppliers have over pricing and terms), the bargaining power of buyers (how much leverage customers have to drive down prices or demand more), the threat of substitutes (how easily customers can switch to alternative solutions), and competitive rivalry (the intensity of competition among existing players). Together, these forces determine the profit potential of an industry and where a company should focus its strategic efforts.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Evaluate how a proposed feature shifts the balance of these five forces. A feature that raises barriers to entry (e.g., proprietary technology, regulatory compliance automation) protects the business from new competitors. Features that reduce buyer bargaining power (e.g., unique capabilities unavailable elsewhere) improve pricing power. Features that create substitution barriers (e.g., ecosystem integration, format lock-in) reduce the threat of alternative solutions. Avoid features that intensify rivalry without creating differentiation, as these erode margins for the entire industry.

### Key Questions

- Does this feature raise or lower barriers to entry in our market?
- Does it increase or decrease our customers' bargaining power over us?
- Does it reduce the threat of substitute products?
- Will it intensify or reduce competitive rivalry?
- Does it affect our dependence on any supplier?

### Red Flags

- The feature lowers barriers to entry, making it easier for new competitors.
- It increases buyer power by commoditizing our offering.
- It triggers a feature war that escalates rivalry without improving margins.
- The feature increases supplier dependency without a fallback.

### Green Flags

- The feature raises barriers to entry for potential competitors.
- It reduces buyer leverage by creating unique, hard-to-substitute value.
- It neutralizes a substitute threat.
- It shifts competitive dynamics in a way that favors the company's position.

## 3. Blue Ocean Strategy (Value Innovation)

### Full Explanation

Blue Ocean Strategy, developed by W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne, contrasts "red oceans" (existing markets with fierce competition) with "blue oceans" (new market spaces with no competition). The core tool is the Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create (ERRC) grid, which challenges teams to identify which factors the industry takes for granted that can be eliminated, which can be reduced well below the standard, which should be raised well above the standard, and which factors should be created that the industry has never offered. Value innovation occurs when a company simultaneously pursues differentiation and low cost, breaking the traditional trade-off.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Use the ERRC grid to evaluate whether a feature merely competes within existing parameters or redefines them. A feature that creates a new factor of competition opens blue ocean territory. A feature that only raises an existing factor without eliminating or reducing others is incremental and keeps the company in red ocean competition. The most valuable features are those that simultaneously eliminate costly complexity and create new sources of value, thereby attracting non-customers who previously could not access or did not want existing solutions.

### Key Questions

- Does this feature create a factor of competition that does not exist today?
- What existing features or complexities can be eliminated or reduced to fund this?
- Does this feature appeal to non-customers or only to existing customers?
- Are we differentiating and reducing cost simultaneously, or only one?
- Does the ERRC grid for this feature show meaningful movement on all four actions?

### Red Flags

- The feature only adds to what competitors already offer (pure "raising").
- No factors are eliminated or reduced to compensate.
- The feature targets over-served customers rather than non-customers.
- It increases cost without creating new demand.

### Green Flags

- The feature creates a new category or factor of value.
- It simplifies or eliminates unnecessary complexity.
- Non-customers or underserved segments find new reasons to adopt.
- The value curve diverges meaningfully from competitors.

## 4. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

### Full Explanation

The Jobs-to-be-Done framework, popularized by Clayton Christensen and further developed by Tony Ulwick and Bob Moesta, reframes customer needs as "jobs" that people are trying to get done. A "job" is the progress that a customer seeks in a given circumstance. Jobs have functional dimensions (the practical task), emotional dimensions (how the customer wants to feel), and social dimensions (how the customer wants to be perceived). Customers "hire" products to do these jobs and "fire" them when they fail. Understanding the job reveals the true competitive set, which is often broader than the obvious product category.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Before evaluating a feature, clearly articulate the job it addresses using the format: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]." Evaluate whether the job is important but poorly satisfied today, as this represents the highest-value opportunity. Features that address unmet jobs are breakthrough opportunities. Features that address over-served jobs waste resources. Features that address jobs customers have found workarounds for may face adoption resistance unless the improvement is dramatic. Always validate the job through customer evidence, not internal assumption.

### Key Questions

- What specific job does this feature help the customer accomplish?
- Is this job currently underserved, adequately served, or over-served?
- What are customers currently "hiring" to do this job (including non-obvious substitutes)?
- Does the feature address functional, emotional, or social dimensions of the job?
- Is there direct customer evidence that this job is important and unsatisfied?

### Red Flags

- The feature addresses a job that customers have already solved satisfactorily.
- No clear job statement can be articulated.
- The job is real but affects very few customers.
- The feature is a solution looking for a problem rather than a response to a validated job.

### Green Flags

- A clear, specific job statement with customer evidence.
- The job is important and currently underserved or unserved.
- Customers are using expensive or inconvenient workarounds.
- The feature addresses multiple dimensions of the job (functional + emotional).

## 5. Wardley Mapping

### Full Explanation

Wardley Mapping, created by Simon Wardley, is a strategy tool that maps the components of a value chain on two axes: visibility to the user (y-axis, from invisible infrastructure at the bottom to visible user needs at the top) and evolutionary stage (x-axis, from Genesis through Custom-Built, Product, to Commodity). Every component evolves from left to right over time as it matures, standardizes, and eventually commoditizes. The map reveals where to invest in innovation (genesis/custom), where to leverage existing products (product stage), and where to use commodity services (commodity stage). It also reveals strategic plays like building on commoditized layers to create new genesis-stage capabilities higher in the value chain.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Map the feature and its dependencies to determine evolutionary positioning. A feature that treats a commodity-stage capability as custom-built wastes resources (use an off-the-shelf solution instead). A feature at the genesis stage requires different investment logic, tolerance for uncertainty, and organizational structure than one at the product stage. Evaluate whether the feature builds on commodity layers (efficient) or requires custom infrastructure that could become a liability. Identify where the feature sits relative to the evolutionary curve and whether timing is right.

### Key Questions

- What evolutionary stage is this feature at (Genesis, Custom-Built, Product, Commodity)?
- Are we building custom what we could buy as commodity?
- What components does this feature depend on, and where are they on the evolution axis?
- Is the market ready for this, or are we too early (genesis in a commodity-expectation market)?
- Does this feature commoditize something that enables higher-value innovation?

### Red Flags

- Building custom solutions for problems that have commodity answers.
- The feature depends on genesis-stage components that introduce high uncertainty.
- Evolutionary stage mismatch: treating a commodity need as novel or a novel need as standard.
- The map shows the feature will be commoditized before ROI is achieved.

### Green Flags

- The feature builds on commodity infrastructure to create genesis-stage value.
- Evolutionary positioning matches investment and organizational capability.
- The map reveals a strategic opportunity competitors have not recognized.
- Dependencies are at stable evolutionary stages (product or commodity).

## 6. TAM/SAM/SOM (Market Sizing)

### Full Explanation

TAM/SAM/SOM is a three-tier market sizing framework. TAM (Total Addressable Market) represents the total revenue opportunity available if a product achieved 100% market share globally with no constraints. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) narrows TAM to the segment a company can realistically serve given its business model, geography, and capabilities. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) further narrows to the portion of SAM the company can realistically capture in a defined time period given competition and go-to-market capacity. Market sizing can be done top-down (from industry data), bottom-up (from unit economics and customer counts), or via value-theory (from willingness to pay for value delivered).

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Size the market opportunity a feature unlocks or expands. A feature that increases TAM (by enabling new use cases or geographies) is strategically different from one that improves penetration within existing SOM. Use bottom-up sizing when possible: estimate the number of users affected, the frequency of the job the feature addresses, and the willingness to pay. Compare the incremental revenue opportunity against development and maintenance cost. Features that expand SAM by unlocking new segments are often more valuable than those that marginally improve conversion in existing segments.

### Key Questions

- What is the incremental TAM/SAM/SOM this feature unlocks?
- Does this feature expand our addressable market or improve capture within it?
- What is the bottom-up revenue estimate (users x frequency x willingness to pay)?
- Is the market growing, stable, or shrinking?
- Does the cost of building and maintaining the feature justify the market opportunity?

### Red Flags

- The addressable market for the feature is small and not growing.
- Market sizing relies entirely on top-down assumptions with no bottom-up validation.
- The feature serves a niche within an already-captured SOM.
- Cost of development exceeds realistic revenue capture over a reasonable horizon.

### Green Flags

- The feature unlocks a meaningful new segment (expands SAM).
- Bottom-up and top-down estimates converge on a large opportunity.
- The market is growing, increasing the value of early investment.
- Revenue-to-cost ratio is attractive with conservative assumptions.

## 7. RICE and ICE Scoring

### Full Explanation

RICE and ICE are quantitative prioritization frameworks used to rank features consistently. RICE scores each feature on four dimensions: Reach (how many users are affected in a given period), Impact (estimated effect on each user, typically on a scale of 0.25 to 3), Confidence (percentage reflecting certainty in estimates), and Effort (person-months or equivalent). The RICE score is calculated as (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort. ICE is a simpler variant scoring Impact (1-10), Confidence (1-10), and Ease (1-10, inverse of effort), multiplied together. Both frameworks force explicit, comparable scoring and reduce the influence of opinion-based prioritization.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Score every candidate feature using the same framework to enable apples-to-apples comparison. Be rigorous about defining Reach using actual data (analytics, user counts, support tickets). Calibrate Impact scales consistently across the team. Use Confidence to penalize features based on assumptions rather than evidence. When scores are close, use the other strategic frameworks (7 Powers, JTBD, etc.) as tiebreakers. RICE and ICE are prioritization tools, not strategy tools; they answer "what order" but not "whether" a feature is strategically sound.

### Key Questions

- What is the quantified Reach of this feature (number of users or transactions affected)?
- What level of Impact do we expect per user, and what evidence supports this?
- How confident are we in these estimates (what would change our confidence)?
- What is the realistic Effort including design, engineering, QA, and rollout?
- How does this score compare to other features currently in the backlog?

### Red Flags

- Reach is assumed rather than measured from data.
- Impact is rated high without customer evidence or analogous data.
- Confidence is set high despite the feature being novel or untested.
- Effort is underestimated by ignoring maintenance, edge cases, or dependencies.
- The framework is used in isolation without strategic context.

### Green Flags

- Reach is grounded in real usage data or validated customer segments.
- Impact estimates are supported by experiments, surveys, or comparable launches.
- Confidence levels are honestly calibrated, with low-confidence items flagged for validation.
- Effort includes full lifecycle cost, not just initial development.
- Scores are used alongside strategic frameworks for holistic prioritization.

## 8. Kano Model

### Full Explanation

The Kano Model, developed by Noriaki Kano, classifies product features into categories based on their relationship to customer satisfaction. Must-Have (Basic) features are expected and do not increase satisfaction when present, but cause strong dissatisfaction when absent (e.g., security, reliability). Performance (Linear) features increase satisfaction proportionally to how well they are executed (e.g., speed, storage). Delighters (Attractive) features are unexpected and create disproportionate satisfaction when present, but cause no dissatisfaction when absent (e.g., a surprisingly elegant interaction). Over time, today's delighters become tomorrow's performance features, and eventually must-haves, in a phenomenon called Kano decay.

### Applying to Feature/Product Evaluation

Classify each proposed feature using the Kano categories to understand its strategic role. Must-have features are table stakes: invest enough to meet expectations but do not over-invest, as they yield diminishing returns. Performance features are where execution quality directly translates to competitive advantage: invest proportionally to their competitive leverage. Delighters are where differentiation and customer advocacy are born: these are high-leverage investments but require ongoing innovation as they decay. Use Kano surveys (asking customers both the functional and dysfunctional form of a question) to empirically classify features rather than relying on internal intuition.

### Key Questions

- Is this feature a Must-Have, Performance, or Delighter in the customer's perception?
- If we do not build this, will customers be dissatisfied (must-have) or simply not delighted?
- Are we over-investing in must-haves at the expense of delighters?
- Has this feature category shifted due to Kano decay (was it a delighter that is now expected)?
- Have we validated the classification with actual customer research?

### Red Flags

- Investing heavily in must-haves that only prevent dissatisfaction without creating differentiation.
- Treating a decayed delighter (now a must-have) as if it still creates competitive advantage.
- No delighters in the roadmap, leading to a functionally adequate but uninspiring product.
- Classification is based on internal assumptions rather than customer evidence.

### Green Flags

- A balanced portfolio of must-haves (sufficient), performance features (competitive), and delighters (differentiated).
- Delighters are informed by genuine customer insight and have surprise potential.
- Kano decay is monitored, and formerly delightful features are maintained at must-have cost.
- Customer research validates the classification before heavy investment.

## Framework Interaction Patterns

These frameworks are most powerful when combined. Common pairings include:

- **JTBD + Kano**: Validate that the job is real (JTBD) and classify the feature's satisfaction dynamics (Kano).
- **7 Powers + Porter's Five Forces**: Identify durable advantage (7 Powers) within the context of industry structure (Porter).
- **Blue Ocean + TAM/SAM/SOM**: Confirm that a new market space (Blue Ocean) represents meaningful revenue potential (TAM/SAM/SOM).
- **Wardley Mapping + RICE**: Position the feature strategically (Wardley) before scoring it for prioritization (RICE).
- **JTBD + RICE**: Ground the Impact and Reach scores in validated customer jobs rather than assumptions.

Always start with customer-facing frameworks (JTBD, Kano) to validate demand, then apply structural frameworks (Porter, 7 Powers, Blue Ocean, Wardley) to assess strategic value, and finish with prioritization frameworks (RICE/ICE, TAM/SAM/SOM) to sequence execution.
